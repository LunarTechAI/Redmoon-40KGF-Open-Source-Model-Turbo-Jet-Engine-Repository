## 4. Regulatory & Compliance Landscape

### 4.1 FAA Regulations (United States)

The Federal Aviation Administration governs drone and experimental aircraft operations in the United States through several regulatory frameworks.

#### Part 107 (Commercial Small UAS)

**Key Requirements:**
- Remote Pilot Certificate required
- Visual line of sight (VLOS) operations
- Maximum altitude: 400 feet AGL
- Maximum speed: 100 mph
- Drone registration required (over 0.55 lbs / 250g)
- Remote ID broadcast required (enforcement from March 2024)

**Turbojet UAV Implications:**
- Weight thresholds may require additional authorizations
- Speed capabilities may exceed Part 107 limits
- Noise considerations for populated areas
- Fuel handling requirements

#### Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS)

**Current Status:**
- New legislation requires FAA to establish routine BVLOS policies by September 2025
- Draft regulations expected December 2024
- Case-by-case waivers currently required

**Implications for Turbojet UAVs:**
The extended range capabilities of turbojet-powered UAVs are particularly suited for BVLOS operations, making this regulatory evolution critical for commercial applications.

#### Experimental Category

For experimental aircraft applications:
- Type certification not required
- Airworthiness certification through experimental category
- Operating limitations apply
- Full technical documentation required for certification

### 4.2 EASA Regulations (European Union)

The European Union Aviation Safety Agency implemented comprehensive drone regulations effective January 1, 2024.

#### Category Framework

**Open Category (Low Risk):**
- Subcategories A1, A2, A3
- Weight and proximity restrictions
- Class Identification Labels (C0-C4)
- Maximum height: 120 meters AGL

**Specific Category (Medium Risk):**
- Operations outside Open Category limits
- Requires operational authorization
- Risk assessment mandatory
- Class C5, C6 drones

**Certified Category (High Risk):**
- Passenger or dangerous goods transport
- Full certification required for drone, operator, pilot

#### Turbojet UAV Classification

Neither FAA nor EASA has specific regulatory categories for turbojet propulsion. Classification is based on:
- **Weight:** Thresholds at 250g, 4kg, 25kg determine requirements
- **Operational Risk:** Speed and power capabilities affect risk categorization
- **Purpose:** Commercial, recreational, or governmental use dictates framework

**Practical Implications:**
Turbojet UAVs typically fall into higher-risk categories (EASA Specific or Certified; FAA waivers required) due to:
- Weight (typically >25kg for 40KGF class platforms)
- Speed capabilities
- Fuel handling requirements
- Noise generation
